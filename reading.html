A Handmade Web

Q1: What is the thesis of this article?

Mez Breeze Carpenter's article explores how the "handmade web" reflects a historical and current kind of resistance against the internet's commercialization and standardization. It honors the early web's originality, innovation, and individualism and encourages active participation in the development and policies of online pages.

In the mid to late 1990s, the web was an experiment for amateurs, artists, and creators to express their creativity. These bright, rich, personal, and "under construction" web pages, are like physical handmade print materials that allow users to reach back in time. besides, it gives users access to source code, providing them with an opportunity to engage with the code and blurring the lines between users and writers.

However, enterprise networks dominated by multinational corporations and proprietary platforms have destroyed the "original spirit" of the web. Therefore, Mez Breeze Carpenter believes that in a commercial environment, the "handmade web" is a form of slow and tiny resistance to counter. it keeps the spirit of the early web, that the internet was full of limitless possibilities, but encourages us to reconsider our relationship with the digital domain, calling for more intimate and individualized encounters outside the limits of conventional commercial platforms.

Q2: Carpenter describes the handmade web as a form of resistance. In your own words, what is being resisted?

There are several elements or developments mentioned by the author in this article, that in the digital landscape are being resisted or challenged, such as Commercialization and Standardization of the Web - where the article resists the trend towards a highly commercialized and standardized web environment dominated by multinational corporations, proprietary applications, and content management systems, Homogenization - emphasizing the importance of diverse and experimental web art and writing projects, Loss of Personal Identity - resists the idea that individuals have lost control over their online identities due to the dominance of big social media platforms and commercial websites, Proprietary Technologies - resisting the prevalence of proprietary technologies that limit users' ability to understand and manipulate the web, and etc. In summary, the resistance in this article is primarily directed towards the commercialization, standardization, loss of personal identity, and proprietary nature of the modern web.

What Screens Want

Q1: What is the thesis of this article?

Frank Chimero's article "What Screens Want," revolves around the distinctive nature of screens, especially digital screens, and their inherent potential for transformation. screens should not be merely static surfaces for displaying information; instead, they should be regarded as interactive materials for designing interfaces. he emphasizes the significance of comprehending the "grain" of screens, which is their fundamental capacity for change and movement. designers should harness this dynamic quality effectively.

By giving examples of the debate "whether flat design and skeuomorphism are better", highlighting the aesthetic neutrality of the screen as a carrier. In addition, he also explained that Movement, change, and animation are not just beautiful, but should be used as a function to help users refine information by giving examples of the three levels of flux: low, high, and medium. The design community needs some precise terminology to help stakeholders communicate and understand interface design.

Finally, he also mentioned the extensibility, openness, and community features of technology, the internet, and commercial interests, encouraging designers to pay attention to and design more impactful and meaningful digital interfaces.

Q2: Where do you stand with the two ideological camps: flat and skeuo?

The choice between skeuomorphic and flat design depends on the specific medium in question. Traditional screens like those on mobile phones and web pages have transitioned from skeuomorphism to flat design over time. Flat design is characterized by minimalism, cleanliness, and consistency, which works well for these platforms. However, in emerging interfaces like AR, VR, and smart home appliances, there's a greater emphasis on familiarity. Skeuomorphic design plays a crucial role in helping users understand and interact with these interfaces by simulating real-world elements.

Although today is an era where flat design is prevalent, I always believe that all “trend”or “style” are cyclical. Just like fashion and typography, design trends undergo cycles where once-abandoned styles eventually regain popularity when people tire of the prevailing aesthetic.

Q3: What is a zoopraxiscope and how does it relate to web and interaction design? Find another example from filmmaking or another medium that has inspired digital design.

It was an early animation device invented by Eadweard Muybridge in the late 19th century. It is used to create the illusion of motion by displaying a series of still images in rapid succession. The present invention emphasizes the concept of conveying movement and change through a series of discrete frames or images.

In digital design, animations, transitions, and interactive elements are essential to guide users into a dynamic and engaging experience. Designers use these techniques to provide feedback, enhance user understanding, and create visually appealing interfaces.

Another example of film production that inspires digital design is the concept of "storyboarding." In filmmaking, storyboards are used to visually plan and outline the sequence of shots and scenes. This technique has been adopted in UX design as a method of planning and visualizing user interactions and interface layout. Designers create wireframes or sketches to outline a user's journey through a digital product or website.

Interface and Interpretation

Q1: How does Drucker define the term "interface"? How about the process of designing interfaces? What is her opinion of interface design processes as they are generally handled today?

According to Johanna Drucker, an interface is a structure that mediates interactions and duties between human users and complicated protocols in digital systems. Users interact with the system there, and the system replies through its processes in this area. Drucker emphasizes that the interface actively influences user behaviors and activities rather than being a passive instrument.

Her criticisms of modern interface design methodologies highlight their mechanical and task-oriented nature, placing an emphasis on effectiveness and user happiness while ignoring the user's dynamic role in meaning construction. She contends that these systems do not take humanistic viewpoints, cultural knowledge, or preferences into account. She advocates for a more constructivist, subject-oriented strategy that acknowledges users' active involvement in interface interactions. This method moves away from one-size-fits-all solutions and takes into account the wide range of user behaviors and contextual complications in order to produce more meaningful and culturally aware digital experiences.

Q2: What is Drucker's notion of "Humanistic Design"?

Design must emphasize the human experience, cultural context, and interpretive interaction over solely utilitarian or technological factors. Interfaces are emphasized as dynamic environments for inquiry, interpretation, and cultural reflection in humanistic design. It urges interfaces to be more than merely useful tools; they should encourage critical thought, welcome ambiguity, and honor the diversity of human expression. Drucker is an advocate of interfaces that offer chances for deep involvement, critical thought, and a variety of viewpoints while also reflecting the complexity of human thought and culture. This method pushes designers to go beyond efficiency and utility to build interfaces that inspire, challenge, and enhance the lives of users. It encourages designers to think about the ethical, cultural, and human components of their work. Instead of only being a source of information, humanistic design aims to make technology a vehicle for the expression of the human spirit.

Q3: As a process, what factors do you feel are central to the design of interfaces that are missing from this article?

Firstly, it doesn't discuss how aesthetics and visual design play a role in the user experience, which has a big impact. Aesthetics, such as color schemes, fonts, and overall visual appeal, are crucial for capturing users' attention and efficiently delivering information. Second, responsiveness is crucial for current interfaces in the era of varied devices. The need to make sure interfaces adapt naturally to different screen sizes and resolutions is disregarded in the text. For the best user experiences, distinct layout and design considerations are needed for desktops, tablets, and mobile devices. Finally, inclusivity and accessibility In many places, it is not only the law but also morally required that interfaces be made usable by people with impairments. To make interfaces accessible to everyone, regardless of their ability, elements like screen reader compatibility, text replacements for graphics, and keyboard navigation must be included.

Database as Symbolic Form

Q1: How does Manovich see the opposition between database and narrative? Manovich talks about computer games as a form of Interactive Narrative. Can you speculate on other forms of interactive narratives (in our culture or imagined) that aren't computer games?

According to Manovich, a key component of modern media and cultural expression is the conflict between the database and the narrative. He recognizes that databases, which provide structured information organization and retrieval, play a fundamental role in contemporary storage mediums. On the other hand, narrative is ingrained in human society and satisfies our desire for meaningful, cogent stories. Manovich suggests investigating their synergy to create novel narrative forms rather than trying to eliminate this opposition.

The emergence of AR and VR technology has enabled users to explore historical events and cultural phenomena in a non-linear manner, providing them with access to extensive databases of data and firsthand narratives. Virtual reality experiences, immersive storytelling installations, and interactive museum displays enable audiences to interact with stories in unique and engaging ways. These interactive narrative formats show how technology may improve storytelling in a variety of ways, going beyond the realm of computer games.

Q2: Database as Symbolic Form was written 19 years ago now. What has changed? What hasn't changed? Did Manovich's predictions come true?

In contemporary culture, databases are becoming more and more important. The relationship between narratives and databases continues to influence how new media and cultural products are produced.

Social media sites like Facebook and Instagram have become so popular that they have grown enormous user bases and changed how people communicate and share their lives. Databases and user experience are becoming even more integrated with streaming services like Spotify and TikTok, which can now offer tailored suggestions based on user data.

While the technological landscape and the cultural applications of databases have broadened, Manovich's findings regarding the friction between the database and story and the preservation of certain aspects of culture remain pertinent.

Laurel Schwulst prefers people to have their own websites instead of relying on social media. She thinks it's important because it gives individuals control, privacy, and the ability to express themselves freely. In a time where a few big companies control social media, having personal websites lets people contribute to a more diverse online world. This situation brings to mind I saw many users on social media complaining about website-building platforms like Wix and Webflow. When users stop paying, these platforms clear all the data from the websites they've created. While these platforms make it easy for users to build their own websites, concerns about privacy and ownership arise.
